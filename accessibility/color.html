Colorblindness in web design

“Colorblind” is itself a misnomer; a more correct term would be “color vision impairment.” However, it has a significant effect on web design color palettes by limiting the range of colors that provide effective contrast while still being visually pleasing.

Name Form of impairment How it affects vision
Protanopia Absence of red retinal photoreceptors
  • Difficuly distinguishing colors in the green-yellow-red area of the spectrum
  • Brightness of red, orange, and yellow colors is reduced
Deuteranopia Absence of green retinal photoreceptors
  • Difficuly distinguishing colors in the green-yellow-red area of the spectrum
Tritanopia Absence of blue retinal photoreceptors
  • Difficult distinguishing blue from green, and yellow from violet

Choosing accessible color palettes

When selecting a color palette for a design, try to go for effective contrast using hue, saturation, lightness, complimentary colors, etc. that is still visible even when color vision impaired.

Contrast

A very important aspect of color schemes is making sure there’s appropriate contrast between color elements. The WCAG recommends various contrast ratios for better visual accessibility; higher ratios mean text is easier to read.

Use Lea Verou’s Contrast Ratio tool for checking contrast ratios between various background and text colors. It updates in realtime to reflect your chosen colors, the contrast ratio, and how it will appear in text on a background.

Best practices

Use neon colors sparingly. While the use of neon colors can feel hip, they are often hard on a user’s eyes.

Avoid vibrating colors. Vibrating colors result from pairing two colors with high saturation together that may be complementary to one another. It creates a glowing or moving effect, which also can be hard on one’s eyes. For example, the red and green pairing that is common with Christmas cards is very glaring when presented on a screen: These are vibrating colors.

Use backdrops or outlines to separate vibrating colors. Separating or reducing vibrating colors with a white or black outline/backdrop can make the effect more bearable for users. For example: ▮

Avoid color combinations with insufficient contrast, including bright colors on top of bright colors (bright on bright), light colors on top of light colors (light on light), and dark colors on top of dark colors (dark on dark).
