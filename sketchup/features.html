The basics

Mouse usage

To use the mouse correctly, do not click and drag during commands. Click once, move the cursor, then click again to complete the command.

Rotate the view by clicking the scroll wheel and dragging while clicked.

Zoom in and out by rolling the scroll wheel.

Pan on the viewing plane by holding shift, clicking the scroll wheel, and dragging while clicked.

Single click to select just a point, edge, or face. Double click to select a face and its bounding edges. Triple click to select any connected geometry.

Drag the mouse from left to right to select all objects completely inside the selection window. Drag the mouse from right to left to select all objects within and overlapping the selection window.

Hold Shift to add or subtract from a selection. Hold Ctrl/Option to only add to the selection, and hold Ctrl/Option plus Shift to only subtract from the selection.

Snapping to points/edges/etc

When moving an object, the refernce point is the point you clicked on. If you want to snap an edge to another edge, a midpoint to another point/midpoint, start the move command from that entity to enable snapping.

Units

SketchUp can understand almost any variation of dimension in a variety of units, fraction or decimal. Each file has a default unit specified; for this unit, you can simply enter numbers. For any other unit, to convert, you’ll need to specify the unit. Note: fractions only work with the default unit.

Example: with a default unit of millimeters, you can simply type 10 for 10mm. To input 1cm, you’ll need to include cm to trigger unit conversion correctly.

Change units through Window > Model info > Units.

Shapes

Lines

Click once to place the first point of a line, move the cursor in the direction you want the line to go (either inferenced or freeform), and either click to place the second point, or type a dimension and press enter.

Circles

Click once to place the center point, move the cursor to define the radius (either inferenced or freeform), and either click to place the radius reference point, or type a dimension and press enter.

Circles are actually made up of a number of lines; the default is 24. This can be changed up or down, but adding more sides will add more load on the program. The key difference between sided circles and polygons is that the circle is rendered with smooth sides when extruded into a cylinder, and the polygon has discrete faces.

Polygons

Click once to place the center point, move the cursor to define the radius (either inferenced or freeform), and either click to place the radius reference point, or type a dimension and press enter.

To change how many sides the polygon has while drawing it, type 8s (for 8 sides) and press enter. You can also do this immediately after drawing the shape.

Use Ctrl/Option to toggle whether the polygon is circumscribed inside (cursor indicates distance to corner) or outside (cursor indicates distance to midpoint of edge) the reference circle.

Resize circles and polygons with the Move tool. Make sure nothing is selected, then hover the Move tool over an endpoint on one of the sides until you find a point that highlights just that point. You can now drag the defined radius in and out to change the size of the shape.

Arcs

There are a few different types of arcs; they all take three clicks to define, and the icons are fairly self-explanatory for how the points affect the final arc.

Use the Entity Info palette to change the number of sides or radius of an arc while it’s still flat.

Center-point arcs are defined by clicking to place the center, clicking again to set the radius, and clicking a third time to set the length of the arc. This tool will snap to quarters of a circle and also displays a protractor while drawing.

Two-point arcs are defined by clicking to place each end point, then clicking again to set the bulge or the distance between the line connecting the endpoints and the high point of the curve. This tool will snap to a half-circle as a handy reference.

You can also draw tangent arcs by using the cyan guide indicator; this works for drawing arcs tangent to lines as well as other arcs. Arcs tangent to two edges will turn magenta instead of cyan. To repeat tangent arcs (for example, filleted corners), simply double-click on the next corner to repeat that arc.

Three-point arcs are defined by clicking to place the starting point, then the pass-through point, then the other endpoint. SketchUp will generate an arc that connects all three of these points. This tool will snap to quarters of a circle.

The Pie tool works just like a center-point arc but adds radial lines to define a pie-slice shape from the defined arc. This tool will snap to quarters of a circle and also displays a protractor while drawing

Rectangles

Click once to place the first point, then move the cursor diagonally to define which direction the rectangle will go in. They try to align with the plane most parallel to the screen; orbit the viewing angle as necessary to get a rectangle on the correct plane. You can either click to place a second point, or type in dimensions (example: 2,4 for a 2' x 4' rectangle).

Rotated rectangles

Click once to place the first point, move the cursor and either enter a dimension or click to place the second point. This is now your “hinge” line, and the third point you place in three-space will define the angle of rotation of the rectangle. When defining degrees, don’t include a unit (example: 2',30 for a 2' wide rectangle rotated 30°).

Freehand

This tool lets you place an edge along a hand-drawn path. Best with a tablet screen. Use this by clicking and holding the left mouse button down while drawing.

Offset

This allows you to expand or contract a contiguous series (loop) of edges. Click once to select the surface inside the edges, then move the cursor and click again to define the offset, or type in a dimension and press enter. Double-click to apply the same direction and magnitude of offset to another surface. You can also select specific edges and apply an offset to them; edges must be in a contiguous loop for this to work.

Eraser

This tool only works on edges; it won’t erase surfaces. To erase a surface, select the surface, then press delete on the keyboard. To select multiple entities easily, click, hold the mouse button down, then scrub over the entities you want to delete to highlight them. Release the mouse button to complete the command.

Hide an edge by holding shift while clicking on an edge.

Soften an edge by holding Ctrl/Option while clicking on an edge.

Moving, grouping, & copying

When moving an entity, reference lines show up to guide you along specific axes. These are usually dashed and the color of the axis they reference.

To copy an entity while moving it, tap the Ctrl key on PC or Option on Mac once. Tap again to toggle copying off.

Groups & components

Group individual entities that you need to move or edit as a unit. If you don’t group entities, you may end up with geometry “sticking” to other entities in ways you didn’t intend.

Make components out of entities that you’ll be copying as a unit, or any entities you commonly use. Components allow you to update all copies of that component within the model just by editing one of them. To break the link to other copies of the same component, right-click on the component and select Make unique.

To edit a component or group, double-click on it with the Select tool. Single-click outside the component to close editing that component. Editing one instance of a component will update all instances of it within the model.

Arrays

Start an array by copying a component, then typing in a multiplier such as “4x”. This will create additional copies of the component (four of them in total including the first, for 4x) along the same movement path, at the offset specified, and in the number that was entered.

You need to use the array function immediately after beginning the first copy of the component. Clicking away from the component will close out the array function and you will need to start over.

Adding dimensions

To precisely dimension an entity, type dimensions immedately after placing the last node of the entity and press enter. For multi-dimension entities such as rectangles, separate dimensions with a comma.

Changing dimensions after the fact

This is oddly tricky in SketchUp compared to most other CAD programs. The easiest way to do this is via guides and scaling: use the measuring tape tool to add guides at the exact distance/location you want to resize the entity to. You can place guides from either the axes, or a reference point on the entity itself. To place a guide parallel to an edge, click the edge, drag the mouse to the general area the guide should be, then enter its dimension. Then, use the scaling tool to resize the entity and snap to the guide.

For reference while resizing and moving, you can use the Dimension tool to display the current dimensions of a specific entity or part of an entity.

Inferenced dimensions

When editing an entity, click once to start the edit command (move, push/pull, etc.). Once you’ve started the command, you can then move the cursor to any other part of the model geometry. This allows easy inferences and snapping to other entities.

To reference another point and place an entity relative to that point (for example, drawing a circle to float above a square), hover over the inference point, then move the cursor to allow snapping to axis inferences from that point.

Push-pull tool

The Push-Pull tool creates extruded bosses or cuts from a face out of or into a surface. To push or pull to a specific depth, click once on the face, move the mouse in the correct direction, then type in the measurement and press enter. To match another point, edge, or face, start the command, then move the cursor to the inference point.

Note that typed-in depths are magnitude and are therefore always positive.

The tool remembers the last depth used. To push/pull entities to the same depth, simply double-click on the next entity. This will push or pull that surface the same depth and in the same relative direction (in or out).

Fillets & Chamfers

Create simple fillets and chamfers on an edge or face by drawing the profile of the fillet or chamfer and using the Follow Me tool.

  1. Draw the fillet/chamfer profile on one of the side faces
  2. Select the top face
  3. Select the Follow Me tool
  4. Click the fillet/chamfer profile
