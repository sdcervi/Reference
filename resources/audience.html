Identify who the site is for

Digital and print designs (brochures, websites, apps, etc.) should always be designed for the target audience—not just for yourself. This makes identifying your target audience essential to producing an effective and useful design. Here are some possible audience traits:

  • Locale: Is the design intended for a small local audience, statewide, countrywide, worldwide...? Other locales could be more context-based, such as urban or rural, big city or small town.
  • Age range: This can be either the age in years, or stage in life.
  • Gender: Male, female, nonbinary, transgender, etc. This may be particularly relevant for social activism or health and medical content.
  • Language: Designs can be adapted easily to different spoken and written languages, but the primary language of your users may govern some design decisions. These can include page weighting left or right, depending on the reading direction of the primary language.
  • Income: Lower-income users will have a different perception of price, value, and opportunity cost than higher-income users. For example, deep sale markdowns on an ecommerce site will appeal to thrifty and budget-conscious users.
  • Education: Tailoring your content to a lower or higher level of education will help it be more relatable and digestible. Technical documents can use more jargon, but blogs should be written more for a general audience, and usually with a more standard vocabulary.
  • Marital or family status: Adults caring for children will have different browsing and shopping habits than those without.
  • Accessibility: Users and readers with disabilities and impairments will need to interact with your content in different ways. If you expect a large portion of your users to be using assistive devices and software, build your designs to work effectively with those systems.
  • Occupation: Whether it’s a passion or simply a job to pay the bills, this can affect how a user or reader interacts with certain types of content.
  • Hours worked per week: People that work long hours and/or long weeks will often not have as much time or attention span to read content. Consider formatting your content as easily digestible, small chunks broken up by logical headings to make it quicker for these users to visually scan your designs.
  • Web usage: People who are on the web a lot tend to be more up to date on memes, trends, and current news.
  • Device: Based on web usage statistics, most users aren’t using the latest, fastest Mac computers with Retina displays and multiple monitors. The data skews more toward 1080p Windows laptops, Android phones, and iPhones around five to six years old.
  • Technical competency: Very few users and readers have the same level of technical experience, knowledge, and skill as the designer. Make sure designs follow a logical flow and are straightforward to use for someone who may struggle with what you consider basic tasks on a device.

Even if your audience covers a broad array of different traits, it can still help to identify specific use cases or user profiles. Telling stories of how a specific user may nagivate your design can call attention to errors you may not have otherwise seen.
