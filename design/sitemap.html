Sitemaps

Organizing your site’s information effectively will make it easier for users to navigate and understand it. The best way to do this is to create a list or diagram that illustrates the structure of the site; this is a site map (also called sitemap). These are also often required for high SEO scores, either in XML or HTML format.

Card sorting

The simplest way to decide on your information hierarchy is to, digitally or on paper, put each piece of information on a card, and sort them into groups. Groups can have sub-groups and sub-sub-groups within those. This card-sorted diagram is then turned into a sitemap.

Sometimes, it can be helpful to bring users in to help you arrange the sitemap. A logical arrangement to you may not make sense to your users, and vise versa.

Page levels

Level 1 or L1 is usually the homepage, or other top-level page in the hierarchy.

Level 2 or L2 pages are the top-level sub-pages, usually large sections that then link to subcategories or individual product pages.

Level 3 or L3 pages are typically subcategories or product pages, nested under an L2.

Wireframes

Wireframes are low-fidelity (simple) mockups to illustrate page structure and layout, without distractions such as colors, images, or other styling. They’re often simple sketches created early in the design process to help guide the team or designer as they create the product. These help make sure that all the information that needs to be on the page is included, and helps define a visual hierarchy for all page elements. Consider them like the scaffodling and then frame that goes up before builders begin constructing the building itself: without a sturdy, well-defined frame, the building won’t function as intended.

Presenting a wireframe to a client allows them to more easily focus on information and functionality; presenting high-fidelty mockups or a final product will often cause them to get stuck on how it looks and feels rather than how it functions. It’s best to limit actual content to headings to define page sections; if you need to put copy text into the wireframe, stick with either Lorem ipsum or public domain classic literature.

Wireframes can be hand-drawn on a whiteboard, paper, or tablet, created using a layout application, or other graphics editing app. Software purpose-built for wireframing websites and apps can often put in buttons and links to help a client navigate through the wireframe just like they will in the final product, which can expose any navigation or information flow issues before you even start writing any code. If a user can’t figure out how to get where they want to go with a black-and-white wireframe, it won’t matter what colors or interface icons you use.

Some good options for creating wireframes:

  • Whiteboard and dry-erase markers
  • Pencil and paper
  • Balsamiq: wireframes resemble sketches and use a library of reusable components, making it clear to clients that this is not a finished product.
  • Figma: this is more of an all-in-one design tool that connects everyone in a team together, and can manage the whole process from brainstorming with flow charts to creating wireframes and sending designs for review. The main selling point is that it enables teams to collaborate on designs in real time.
  • Adobe XD: this is the Adobe Creative Cloud answer to UI/UX design needs for wireframes and robust prototyping tools. The main benefit is that it integrates natively with the other Creative Cloud applications such as Photoshop and Illustrator.
