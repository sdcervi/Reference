What is whitespace?

Whitespace, also referred to as white space, negative space, empty space, or breathing room, is simply the empty area around design elements. Despite its name, whitespace is not always white; it’s whatever background color you have in your design.

Too much whitespace, and a design can feel disconnected and empty. However, good use of large amounts of whitespace can help focus a design. For example, the Google homepage makes excellent use of whitespace to focus users on the primary task: searching the internet.

Too little whitespace, and a design can feel crowded, busy, and difficult to read. Think of a webpage, email, or poster that felt like a wall of text; these designs are visually overwhelming and offputting.

Types of whitespace

Different types of whitespace have different effects on a design. They can be used to improve readability, prioritize content sections, and visually group content together.

Type Definition Usage
Passive The space used to enhance a page’s aesthetics, without guiding a reader through a specific reading, flow, or content order.
  • Letter spacing
  • Line spacing
Active The space used to enhance a page’s structure and help guide a user through the content.
  • Macro white space
  • Grouping elements together into content blocks
  • Emphasizing primary content
  • Paragraph spacing
  • Inset, blockquote, and floated image spacing
Macro The space between major layout elements and sections, and the space surrounding the entire design layout. Macro whitespace acts as a container for the overall design. It decreases the work the user has to do to use your website.
  • To the left and/or right of a site’s main content
  • Between content blocks
Micro The space between design elements. This has a direct impact on legibility.
  • Between lines of text and paragraphs
  • Between grid images
  • Between menu links

How much whitespace should I use?

This depends on several factors.

Content: content-heavy layouts will have less macro whitespace, and rely more heavily on micro whitespace.

Design: the user interface design influences the ratio between macro and micro whitespace in a layout. Designers will usually favor a particular design style.

User: user experience research and testing can help inform whether a current layout is working, and if not, how to improve it. Different audiences will have different expectations and needs.

Branding message: luxury, high-end brands will usually favor large amounts of macro whitespace, to emphasize their product. High-quality news websites will often reduce whitespace to mimic the style of paper newspapers.
