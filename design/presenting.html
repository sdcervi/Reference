Presenting Design Work

  • Home

Presenting isn’t actually about presenting. It’s about listening, communicating, inviting others to gain ownership in your designs, and coming up with a better final product as a team. Remove ego from the equation and focus on the product. Criticism is what makes the end product better, even harsh criticism.

How presentations should work

  • The audience understands what’s happening and what’s expected of them
  • Stakeholders listen, ask smart, relevant questions, and give feedback related to their expertise (instead of opinion)
  • You feel calm, confident, and in control
  • The entire team walks away able to create a much better product

Types of presentations

  • Progress check-ins
    • Usually happen for long-running projects
    • Show progress and solicit feedback and input
    • Listen more than you present
  • Showcases
    • More formal
    • Show current state of project (not progress) and collect quality feedback
    • Focus on problems being solved and not the details
  • Final sign-off
    • Don’t present anything new
    • Any feedback here will inform a follow-up phase
    • Summarize all previous work

Preparing the audience

  • Give people a job to do relevant to their role in the project. Give everyone something specific to think about and emphasize that you want their expertise.
    • “Will this design work for the core user group?”
    • “Will this design work for the content?”
    • “Is there terminology users won’t understand?”
    • “Are there edge cases and users that this design won’t work for?”
    • “Does this adhere to brand and style guidelines?”
  • Explain the feedback process
    • Questions and comments at the end; there will be time for this
    • Let them know you’ll listen and value their contribution
  • Set the context
    • Define in and out of scope for presentation

Planning the presentation

  • Present in the lowest-fidelity state that will achieve the presentation’s goals (i.e. wireframes)
  • Use obviously fake content for filler to showcase layout, but steer away from Lorem ipsum since it can confuse people (i.e. Alice in Wonderland and other public-domain texts)
  • Delegate technology control and note-taking to someone else so you can focus on presenting; consider recording so no one has to take notes and attendees can re-watch if they missed anythingh

The presentation itself

  • Explain the problem you’re solving or goals you’re trying to meet
    • Multiple perspectives: end user and supervision/management
  • Start with the end result, not the process to get there
  • Tell a story of the user interacting with your product, multiple stories if needed for multiple use cases
  • Don’t use jargon

Feedback

Good feedback is:

  • Concrete
  • Actionable
  • Specific (with examples)
  • Detailed and clear
  • Based on the person’s expertise

You may have to train your audience in how to give good feedback.

  1. Remind them of scope: “Remember, today we’re focusing on [subject]. We’ll go through [other subject] next time.”
  2. Remind them of their jobs, and ask each person specific questions relevant to them
  3. Add some general questions
    • “What are you most looking forward to” to prompt positive feedback
    • “Is there anythign else that could become a problem?”
    • “Was there anything you expected to see today that I didn’t cover?”
  4. As people to provide examples to support their comments

One good method of making sure people feel heard with “questions at the end” is to give them sticky notes and have them write down questions as they come up. Put all the sticky notes on a wall at the end, group them thematically, and then discuss.
